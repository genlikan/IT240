What is WebGL?

In short, is a JavaScript API that allows for GPU-accelerated usage.

But technological descriptions are esoteric in nature, time to showcase some popular applications of WebGL.

Here is one of, if not the most popular examples associated with WebGL technology.

Made by Evan Wallace, this WebGL demo requires a decent modern graphics card to execute. If you don’t see it on here, you may refer to this link on YouTube.

From here you may interact with the demo,

Try:
Clicking on the water to make ripples
Dragging the background to rotate the camera
Dragging the sphere to move it around

More interactions are availiable on his page.
This demo makes an impressive case for illustrative applications of WebGL.

Another application of WebGL is Data Visualization.

The above demonstration - created by Aodhan Sweeney - denotes the rising tempratures over the past few decades.

Humans are intuitive creatures.

There exist instances where using numbers and symbols simply isn't enough to carry the message or the point forward.
Often when we are attempting to illustrate a point via numbers, percentages or other symbols, we typically fail to grasp the degree of significance or the scale in which an issue could impact us.

Enter WebGL.

WebGL technology enables us to (interactively) visualize data points to aid in illustrating key points.

Data Visualizations isn't just limited to Global Climate change awareness.

It can also be used in the Medical field.

The above demo, as quoted from SliceDrop shows:

"The surface of the left hemisphere of a 2 years old healthy brain."
Developed by a fellow University of Massachusetts Boston Professor Daniel Haehn.
SliceDrop - is a tool to conceptualize the human brain and other medical applications in interactive 3D space.
This tool also features realtime collaboration.

So is this technology hard to use?

Surprisingly simple!
